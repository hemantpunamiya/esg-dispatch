BRSR FY26 — PSU Bank
UCO Bank Files FY26 BRSR With Net Zero Transition Plan Aligned to Government of India — Scope 1 & 2 Net Zero by 2050, Full Net Zero (Operations + Financed Portfolio) by 2056, 51,615 tCO₂e Operational Footprint, 20,993 Employees, 29.53% Female Representation
UCO Bank on 22 May filed its Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report for FY 2025–26 with NSE and BSE under SEBI (LODR) Regulations, 2015. The bank adopted a Net Zero Transition Plan aligned with the Government of India's climate commitments, targeting net zero across both operations and the financed portfolio by 2056, with Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions targeted for net zero by 2050. Reported energy consumption of 265,277 GJ (8.93 GJ per crore of turnover) and combined Scope 1+2 emissions of 51,615.41 tCO₂e (1.74 tCO₂e per ₹1 crore revenue). Water consumption of 236,070 kilolitres. Workforce of 20,993 employees with 29.53% female representation. Standalone turnover ₹29,705 crore; net worth ₹24,462 crore.
Source: ScanX / UCO Bank FY26 BRSR filing / NSE-BSE filings (22 May 2026)
Why it matters for corporate ESG: A formal Net Zero Transition Plan from a mid-tier PSU bank — aligned explicitly to the Government of India's climate commitments — embeds financed-portfolio decarbonisation in the disclosure architecture rather than confining it to a non-binding sustainability webpage. For Indian PSU-bank peers (PNB, Canara, Union Bank, Bank of India, Bank of Maharashtra, Indian Bank, Central Bank, Punjab & Sind Bank), the UCO template — Net Zero Transition Plan + Scope 1+2 by 2050 + full portfolio net zero by 2056 + per-crore emissions intensity disclosure — becomes the disclosure floor that RBI's evolving climate-risk framework will measure against during the next supervisory review cycle. For private-bank peers (HDFC, ICICI, Axis, Kotak, IndusInd, IDFC First) the forward-looking financed-emissions disclosure becomes a comparability anchor. For BRSR Principle 6 in banking, the UCO commitment is the kind of forward-looking financed-emissions disclosure auditors will reference when reviewing FY27 BRSRs.

Energy Policy
Ministry of Power Releases Draft National Electricity Policy 2026 — First Refresh in 21 Years; Nuclear Eligible for Green Bond Funding, Retired Thermal-Plant Sites Earmarked for Nuclear Repurposing, Brownfield Coal-to-Nuclear Conversion Considered Where Feasible
India's Ministry of Power released the Draft National Electricity Policy 2026 during the week — the first comprehensive refresh of the country's national electricity policy since the original 2005 NEP. Key headline elements include explicit eligibility of nuclear power projects for Green Bond funding, brownfield expansion of existing nuclear sites prioritised over greenfield, replacement of coal-based captive plants with nuclear where feasible, and repurposing of retired thermal-plant sites for nuclear power "wherever feasible." The draft aligns with the FY26 Union Budget's nuclear ambitions and the long-stated 500 GW renewables-by-2030 plus 100 GW nuclear-by-2047 targets.
Source: World Nuclear News / Ministry of Power / Draft National Electricity Policy 2026 (May 2026)
Why it matters for corporate ESG: Bringing nuclear inside Green Bond eligibility is the single most consequential domestic-financing-architecture change for the Indian nuclear sector since the 2010 Civil Liability Act. For ESG-screened EM-debt buyers (Norges Bank, GPIF, AP funds, PGGM, ABP, CPP Investments, BlackRock EM debt) nuclear-tagged green bonds become a permissible inclusion category for India exposure where they were previously excluded under stricter taxonomy interpretations. For Indian PSU energy companies (NTPC, NTPC Green Energy, NPCIL, BHEL, L&T Heavy Engineering, Hindustan Construction) the policy unlocks a new class of green-bond financing for nuclear capex. For private players potentially entering nuclear after the previously-passed liability-act amendments (Reliance Industries, Tata Power, Adani Power, JSW Energy, Vedanta) the brownfield-coal-to-nuclear pathway is a directly actionable strategic option. For BRSR Principle 6 disclosure in Indian power and utilities, nuclear-aligned transition-plan disclosure becomes a comparability anchor over FY27–28. For the broader Indian taxonomy debate, the draft policy is the first formal central-government signal that nuclear is "transition-aligned" rather than "fossil-adjacent."

The Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEFCC) during the week notified the Plastic Waste Management (Amendment) Rules, 2026, introducing stricter third-party audit requirements for entities operating under Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) obligations. The amendment tightens traceability across producer-importer-brand-owner categories and the registered plastic-waste-processor ecosystem. The rules build on the original 2016 PWM Rules and the 2022 EPR amendment, and run in parallel to the BRSR Principle 6 disclosure architecture that already covers plastic-waste handling.
Source: Mongabay India / MoEFCC notification / Plastic Waste Management Amendment Rules 2026 (May 2026)

SECI Solar+Storage Tariff
SECI Awards 10 MW Solar + 10 MW/20 MWh BESS Auction to BNC Power Projects in Odisha at ₹2.60/kWh — 25-Year PPA, Build-Own-Operate; Sub-₹3 Solar+Storage Lands as the Operational Pricing Baseline for FDRE-Style Indian Tenders
The Solar Energy Corporation of India (SECI) on 18 May announced BNC Power Projects as the winning bidder in its auction to develop a 10 MW grid-connected solar project paired with a 10 MW/20 MWh battery energy storage system in Odisha at a tariff of ₹2.60 per kWh. Source: Renewable Watch / SECI auction notification / Energetica India (18–22 May 2026)

EU Sustainability Reporting
EU Commission's Draft ESRS 2.0 Consultation Runs Through 3 June — 489 Mandatory Datapoints Removed (~60%+ Cut), Voluntary VSME-Type Standard for <1,000 Employees Companies Doubles as Statutory "Value Chain Cap"
The European Commission's draft revised European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS 2.0), published 6 May and open for public consultation through 3 June, continued to shape the disclosure conversation through the 18–24 May window. Headline simplifications: removal of 489 previously mandatory datapoints (Commission states a ~60%+ reduction in mandatory datapoints and ~70%+ total-datapoint reduction), removal of all voluntary datapoints, choice of operational vs financial control as the GHG boundary, primary-microplastics-only disclosure, and a "top-down" materiality approach allowing strategy-anchored analysis. Double materiality remains. The parallel voluntary VSME-type standard for entities with ≤1,000 employees doubles as the statutory "value chain cap." Final delegated act expected June–July; parliamentary scrutiny up to 4 months.
Source: ESG Today / Latham & Watkins / Linklaters / Morrison Foerster / Ropes & Gray / European Commission (consultation open through 3 June 2026)
Why it matters for corporate ESG: The 60%+ mandatory-datapoint cut narrows the global disclosure-architecture gap between ESRS, ISSB IFRS S1+S2, and BRSR Core. For non-EU multinationals with EU branch operations triggering Article 40a (Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Alphabet, Meta, J&J, P&G, Coca-Cola, Toyota, Samsung, Reliance Europe, Tata Steel Europe, JSW Steel Europe, Infosys EU, Wipro EU, TCS EU) the simplification materially lowers the compliance burden of the third-country mechanism. For BRSR Core preparers in India, the simplification means the prior "ESRS is the harder standard, BRSR is the lighter one" framing collapses — BRSR Core's quantitative granularity is now closer to ESRS than at any point since CSRD entered force. The mandatory 1.5°C-alignment-statement requirement is the disclosure mechanism that turns transition-plan credibility into a binary auditable item — companies whose plans aren't 1.5°C-aligned must say so on the record. For ESG-rating users (MSCI, Sustainalytics, S&P Global ESG, ISS-ESG, Moody's ESG, Refinitiv) the consultation outcome shapes 2026–27 methodology revisions.
