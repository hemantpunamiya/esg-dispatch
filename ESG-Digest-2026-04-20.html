India

The carbon market compliance clock starts ticking, grid standards get a 500 GW upgrade, NTPC lights up Rajasthan, and green ammonia finds its government buyer.
Regulatory & Policy "India's carbon market starts its compliance clock — and the grid's new rulebook just got 80+ pages longer."
Carbon Market
India's Carbon Credit Trading Scheme Goes Live: 490 Industrial Plants Now Have Binding Emission Targets
India's Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS) has formally entered the compliance phase for FY2025–26, with approximately 490 entities across seven energy-intensive sectors — aluminium, cement, chlor-alkali, pulp & paper, petroleum refining, petrochemicals, and textiles — now subject to legally binding greenhouse gas emission intensity targets. Companies that outperform their targets earn tradeable Carbon Credit Certificates (CCCs); those that fall short must purchase and surrender equivalent credits. The first compliance deadline is July 31, 2026, covering the FY2025–26 compliance year with FY2023–24 as the emissions baseline. The CCTS is expected to initially cover over 700 million tonnes of CO₂e — placing India among the world's largest emissions trading systems at launch — with the voluntary Offset Mechanism for non-obligated sectors running in parallel. First CCC trading is expected to begin by mid-2026.
Source: ICAP / Bureau of Energy Efficiency / Ministry of Power (April 2026)
Context: The compliance start is the moment India's carbon market stops being a policy document and starts being a financial risk. For the 490 entities in scope: the first compliance year is FY2025–26 — but that year is already nearly over. Companies that haven't tracked emissions against the CCTS baseline since April 2025 are now scrambling to assess whether they'll be buyers or sellers of CCCs by July. The stakes are real: non-compliance carries financial penalties, and the market is still building price discovery mechanisms. For Indian CFOs: the CCTS is structurally similar to the EU ETS in its early years — opaque, incomplete, but undeniably real. The companies that build internal carbon pricing now will have a material competitive advantage when the market deepens and prices rise post-2027. For global investors: India's CCTS at 700 Mt CO₂e coverage is larger than the EU ETS at launch — this is not a minor policy experiment.
Grid Standards
India's CEA Sets Comprehensive New Technical Standards for BESS, Solar, and Wind — Effective April 2027
India's Central Electricity Authority (CEA) issued the Technical Standards for Construction of Electrical Plants and Electric Lines Amendment Regulations, 2026 on 14 April, introducing mandatory technical benchmarks for all new Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS), solar, and wind installations. Key requirements include: BESS systems must retain 90% output after five years and 70% after fifteen; large projects (50 MW+) must have grid-forming inverter technology and black-start capability; solar plants must operate for a minimum 25 years with bypass diode and tracking components; offshore wind installations require seabed scour protection, specialised cable systems, and helipads on substations; onshore wind turbines must be sited at least 500 metres from residential areas. All renewable facilities must support remote load dispatch centre communications and retain operational data for 90 days. The standards take effect from April 1, 2027, giving the industry a one-year transition window.
Source: SolarQuarter / Central Electricity Authority (14 April 2026)
Context: India's 500 GW renewable target by 2030 is an engineering problem as much as a financing one — and this amendment addresses the engineering half. The 90-day data retention requirement plus remote dispatch connectivity means India's distributed renewable fleet will effectively become a monitorable, dispatchable network for the first time. The 500-metre wind setback rule is important for community acceptance — it removes one of the most common local opposition arguments against onshore wind. The 25-year minimum lifespan requirement for solar is a quality floor that should improve bankability for long-tenor green bonds and project finance. For developers: the April 2027 effective date gives one year to redesign specifications on upcoming projects. For equipment suppliers: grid-forming inverters are the dominant technology requirement here — watch for accelerated procurement demand across Adani, ReNew, Greenko, and NTPC Green project pipelines.
Large Company Actions & Big Moves "NTPC lights up Phase 1 in Rajasthan, green ammonia breaks ₹3,700 crore ground with SECI, and 18 hydrogen startups pitch their way to ₹100 crore."
Solar
NTPC Green Energy Commissions 150 MW Solar in Rajasthan, Group Total Crosses 10,276 MW
NTPC Green Energy Limited (NGEL) declared commercial operation of 150 MW of solar capacity from its Rajasthan project effective April 18, 2026. The capacity is held through Project Sixteen Renewable Power Private Limited, a step-down subsidiary of the ONGC-NTPC joint venture. This represents the first phase of a 300 MW solar project, with the remaining capacity continuing toward completion. The commissioning raises NTPC Green Energy Group's total installed capacity to 10,276.40 MW and commercial capacity to 10,126.40 MW — a milestone that puts the subsidiary comfortably past the 10 GW mark. NTPC Group's overall generation capacity, across all fuel sources, reaches 89,528 MW installed. The project was disclosed under SEBI's Regulation 30 listing obligations.
Source: Free Press Journal / NSE / NTPC Green Energy (18 April 2026)
Context: The 10 GW milestone for NTPC Green Energy is more than a round number — it's the threshold at which a renewable subsidiary begins to function as a credible standalone financing platform. NGEL's IPO, which raised ₹10,000 crore in late 2024, committed to aggressive capacity addition; hitting 10 GW validates the investment thesis. The ONGC-NTPC joint venture structure for this project is worth noting: it signals that PSU energy companies are increasingly co-developing renewable assets, pooling expertise and balance sheets. The phased commissioning approach (150 MW first, then the remaining 150 MW) is a bankable strategy that enables earlier revenue recognition. For analysts tracking NTPC Green: watch for the 300 MW full commissioning date and subsequent project announcements, as the pipeline must continue growing to justify the IPO valuation.
Green Ammonia
InSolare Energy and SCC Infrastructure Sign ₹3,700 Crore Green Ammonia Deal with SECI for 85,000 MTPA Supply
InSolare Energy and SCC Infrastructure signed a Government Approval and Purchase Agreement (GAPA) with the Solar Energy Corporation of India (SECI) on 13 April for the supply of 85,000 metric tonnes per annum (MTPA) of green ammonia. The deal, valued at approximately INR 3,700 crore (USD 398 million), is structured under SECI's green ammonia procurement scheme, which is part of India's National Green Hydrogen Mission. Green ammonia — synthesised by combining green hydrogen (produced via electrolysis powered by renewable energy) with nitrogen — is a key maritime fuel, fertiliser feedstock, and hydrogen carrier being targeted under India's green transition roadmap. The agreement provides InSolare and SCC with a guaranteed government offtake — a critical de-risking mechanism for capital-intensive green ammonia projects.
Source: Fuel Cells Works / SECI / Ministry of New and Renewable Energy (13 April 2026)
Context: The SECI GAPA structure is the Indian government's most powerful tool for de-risking green hydrogen and ammonia projects — it functions as a sovereign purchase guarantee, making project finance bankable where private offtake would not be. This ₹3,700 crore deal is significant in absolute size, but the 85,000 MTPA figure is the critical data point: at current green ammonia production economics (~$600–700/tonne), this represents one of the largest committed supply volumes under India's NH₂ mission. The green ammonia value chain — electrolysers, renewable energy, Haber-Bosch plants, storage — requires all components to be built simultaneously, and sovereign offtake makes the financing math work. For fertiliser companies (Coromandel, RCF, IFFCO) watching India's green ammonia pipeline: SECI deals of this scale indicate a government serious about displacing grey ammonia imports, which currently account for ~2.5 Mt/year.
Hydrogen
MNRE Showcases 18 Hydrogen Startups at New Delhi Exhibition; Launches ₹100 Crore "Novel Uses" Fund
India's Ministry of New and Renewable Energy (MNRE) hosted a one-day Hydrogen Startup Exhibition in New Delhi on 16 April, featuring 18 specialised startups representing different segments of the hydrogen value chain. Innovations on display spanned advanced electrolyser systems, fuel cell technology for transport and stationary applications, biomass-to-hydrogen conversion methods, and digital hydrogen management solutions. Principal Scientific Adviser Ajay Sood and MNRE Secretary Santosh Sarangi attended, signalling senior government commitment to commercialising hydrogen innovation. MNRE simultaneously launched the "New and Novel Uses of Hydrogen" scheme with ₹100 crore in funding — individual projects can apply for up to ₹5 crore. The National Institute of Solar Energy has already received 111 proposals under the scheme, with 58 advancing to final evaluation. India's broader hydrogen startup ecosystem has grown to 249 companies as of September 2025, per DPIIT data.
Source: SolarQuarter / MNRE / Fuel Cells Works (16 April 2026)
Context: The ₹100 crore fund is small relative to the capital required to build a hydrogen economy — the InSolare/SECI deal announced three days earlier is 37x larger. But the purpose is different: seed funding for novel applications (hydrogen in aviation, shipping, heavy industry, and distributed power) that wouldn't attract commercial capital yet. This is the government acting as a venture funder for hard-to-scale technology. The 249-startup figure tells the real story: India's hydrogen ecosystem is building rapidly at the company-formation stage, and what it currently lacks is commercial contracts, demonstration scale, and standardised certification. MNRE's exhibition addresses the visibility gap — connecting laboratory-stage startups with procurement teams from NTPC, ONGC, L&T, and IOCL that have hydrogen project pipelines but limited startup engagement channels.
Carbon & Decarbonisation "8,000 tonnes commissioned — 5 million tonnes targeted. India's green hydrogen race has a scoreboard now."
Decarbonisation
India's Commissioned Green Hydrogen Capacity Reaches 8,000 Tonnes Per Year — 0.16% of the 2030 Mission Target
India has commissioned approximately 8,000 metric tonnes per annum (MTPA) of green hydrogen production capacity as of early 2026 under the National Green Hydrogen Mission, according to government data. Commissioned units include electrolysers and associated renewable energy systems at refineries, fertiliser plants, and industrial pilots across Rajasthan, Gujarat, Maharashtra, and Odisha. While the 8,000 MTPA figure represents genuine progress, it underscores the scale challenge: India's 2030 target of 5 million MTPA of green hydrogen — approved under a ₹19,744 crore Mission budget — requires a roughly 625x expansion in production capacity within four years. The Mission's incentive schemes — including the SIGHT incentive for electrolysers and green hydrogen offtake — have generated ₹3,700+ crore in signed SECI GAPAs (including the InSolare deal this week), pointing to an accelerating commercialisation pipeline.
Source: The Industry Outlook / MNRE / National Green Hydrogen Mission (April 2026)
Context: The blunt arithmetic is sobering: at 8,000 tonnes per year today, reaching 5 million tonnes by 2030 requires compounding production at ~200% annually for four consecutive years — a rate of scale-up that no industrial technology has achieved outside wartime mobilisation. The realistic scenario: India will likely commission 100,000–300,000 MTPA of green hydrogen by 2030, not 5 million — which would still represent a 30–40x expansion from today. That is genuinely impressive, but the 5 MMT headline target is increasingly a political goalpost rather than an engineering plan. The key metrics to track: electrolyser manufacturing capacity in India (Greenko, Ohmium, NTPC Green are building), cost of green hydrogen at the plant gate (currently ~₹400/kg vs grey hydrogen at ~₹100/kg), and how many SECI GAPAs get built vs remain paper commitments. The 8,000-tonne baseline is a starting gun, not a victory lap.

Regulatory & Policy "Anti-ESG laws meet First Amendment law firms, EU peatlands get €1.3 billion, and bank ESG reporting gets a proportionality makeover."
Anti-ESG
ISS Sues Indiana Over Anti-ESG Proxy Law, Calling It an Unconstitutional Gag on Investment Research
Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) filed a federal lawsuit on 15 April challenging Indiana's H.B. 1273, which requires proxy advisers to attach disclosures to any recommendation against management that is not grounded in written financial analysis considering short- and long-term costs and benefits. The law, which takes effect July 2026, has been replicated in approximately 12 other US states. ISS argues H.B. 1273 violates the First Amendment by selectively targeting only anti-management recommendations, is unconstitutionally vague, and would effectively compel proxy advisers to make false statements — since "many issues that generally come up for a shareholder vote do not lend themselves to financial prediction." The lawsuit follows a Trump executive order directing federal agencies to oversee proxy advisory firms, and recent investigations by Florida and Texas attorneys general into ISS and Glass Lewis ESG voting practices.
Source: ESG Today / ISS / The Hill (15 April 2026)
Context: This lawsuit is the most significant legal test of anti-ESG state legislation to date. The First Amendment argument is the strongest available: if proxy advisers' recommendations are protected speech (which courts have generally held for financial research), then a law requiring specific disclaimers on one side of an argument — and only that side — is content-based speech regulation subject to strict scrutiny, which the government rarely wins. The political logic of H.B. 1273 is clear: make ESG-aligned proxy advice operationally cumbersome and legally risky. But litigation reveals its constitutional exposure. For pension funds and asset managers in the 12 states that have enacted similar laws: the outcome of this case will determine whether your proxy adviser can continue providing independent research on executive compensation, board diversity, and climate risk, or whether those recommendations are effectively chilled into silence. The global ESG governance community — and Indian institutional investors accessing US markets — should watch this closely.
Climate Finance
EU Approves €5 Billion for German Peatland Rewetting and Czech Biomethane Scale-Up
The European Commission approved two major climate-related state aid schemes on 15 April 2026 totalling approximately €5 billion. Germany's €1.3 billion peatland rewetting programme supports the rehabilitation of drained peatlands as carbon sinks through land rewetting, compensation for agricultural economic losses, and advisory services for farmers transitioning to paludiculture — a form of wet farming compatible with preserved peatlands. Drained peatlands currently account for 7% of annual EU greenhouse gas emissions. The Czech Republic's €3.7 billion biomethane programme will support new biomethane production stations and conversions of existing biogas facilities for transport, heating, and industrial use, targeting 350 million standard cubic metres of annual output by 2030, primarily benefiting SME farms. European Commission Executive Vice-President Teresa Ribera cited both measures as developing "sustainable value chains while taking all relevant stakeholders on board."
Source: ESG Today / European Commission / Clean Energy Wire (15 April 2026)
Context: The German peatland programme is the largest land-use climate intervention in EU history. Peatlands store more carbon per hectare than any other terrestrial ecosystem — drained and farmed peatlands effectively become net emitters, reversing that dynamic is one of the highest-leverage climate actions available in Europe. The €1.3 billion cost must be weighed against the emissions benefit: Germany has approximately 1.8 million hectares of degraded peatland; rewetting them could sequester 20–40 Mt CO₂e per year — a €32–65/tonne abatement cost that is competitive with almost every other decarbonisation pathway. For carbon markets: voluntary peatland restoration credits could become a significant new supply source if the EU creates a standardised certification path. For the Czech biomethane programme: 350 million cubic metres by 2030 represents roughly 3% of Czech gas demand — modest but meaningful for agricultural sector decarbonisation.
Regulation
EBA Proposes Three-Tier ESG Reporting Framework for Banks: Big Banks Get More, Small Banks Get Much Less
The European Banking Authority (EBA) announced on 16 April a major proposed overhaul of its ESG supervisory reporting framework, introducing proportionality through a three-tier system based on institution size. Large institutions (more than €30 billion in assets) and listed banks would face new supervisory templates covering environment-related corporate exposures and risks beyond climate change. Mid-tier listed institutions and large subsidiaries receive a streamlined but meaningful set of disclosures. Small and non-complex institutions (SNCIs) — including most community banks and credit unions — would be limited to a single annual template on climate-related physical and transition risks. The proposal also removes the EU Taxonomy exposure template (BTAR) entirely, which the EBA acknowledged generated significant compliance burden without proportionate supervisory benefit. The public consultation closes July 10, 2026; implementation is targeted for September 2027.
Source: ESG Today / European Banking Authority (16 April 2026)
Context: The removal of the BTAR taxonomy exposure template is the most significant substantive change here. BTAR required banks to calculate and report what fraction of their loan portfolios was "taxonomy-aligned" — a theoretically elegant metric that proved practically unworkable because most corporate borrowers do not themselves report CSRD-grade taxonomy data. The EBA is effectively acknowledging that supervisory reporting cannot outpace corporate reporting maturity. The three-tier proportionality system is also important: previously, a €500 million community savings bank and a €500 billion universal bank faced similar ESG reporting templates — an obvious mismatch. The new structure means smaller European banks can refocus compliance resources on business rather than regulatory paperwork. For Indian private sector banks with European subsidiaries or correspondent banking relationships: this simplification may reduce incoming ESG data requests from European counterparties in 2027–28.
Large Company Actions & Big Moves "Amazon's Australian gigawatt, DHL and IAG fill their jets with cooking oil, a Rivian battery gets a second life on the grid, and BTG Pactual bets $370M on Latin American trees."
Clean Energy
Amazon Signs Nine PPAs in Australia — 430 MW of Renewable Capacity to Power Data Centres, First Solar-Battery Hybrids Outside US
Amazon signed nine power purchase agreements (PPAs) across New South Wales and Victoria on 16 April, delivering 430 MW of new renewable energy capacity across one wind farm, three utility-scale solar-battery hybrid projects, four distributed solar-battery installations, and one battery storage system added to its existing Mokoan solar facility. Partners include OX2, X-ELIO, Anza, TagEnergy, and European Energy. The deals bring Amazon's total renewable capacity in Australia to nearly 1 gigawatt. The company has invested approximately A$2.8 billion (USD 2 billion) in Australian renewable projects since 2020, with plans for an additional A$20 billion in data centre infrastructure investment by 2029. Eight of the nine agreements include co-located battery storage systems — Amazon's first solar-battery hybrid projects outside the United States. Amazon was Australia's largest corporate carbon-free energy purchaser in 2025.
Source: ESG Today / Amazon / AFR (16 April 2026)
Context: Amazon's Australia strategy is a template for how hyperscalers will power the AI buildout in markets outside the US: anchor renewable energy capacity with large PPAs, bundle in battery storage to ensure 24/7 reliability, and structure deals with local developers to avoid grid bottlenecks. The 430 MW across nine deals (average ~48 MW each) is deliberately diversified — not one giant project but a portfolio that mitigates construction and permitting risk. The A$20 billion data centre commitment is the underlying demand driver: that infrastructure will consume roughly 500–700 MW at full load, so Amazon is pre-building its renewable supply now. For Indian renewable developers and power exchanges: the Australian model is directly replicable in India's three major data centre clusters (Mumbai, Chennai, Hyderabad), where hyperscaler PPA demand is growing faster than developers can supply. The co-located solar-battery structure should become standard for Indian data centre PPAs by 2027.
Aviation
DHL and IAG Cargo Sign Five-Year SAF Deal: 240 Million Litres of Cooking-Oil Jet Fuel, 640,000 Tonnes CO₂e Saved
DHL Express and IAG Cargo signed a five-year sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) agreement on 15 April covering approximately 40 million litres of neat SAF annually for DHL Express. Combined with a 2025 renewal, the deal amounts to approximately 240 million litres of SAF uplifted at London Heathrow Airport, covering nearly all fuel used to transport DHL Express cargo across IAG Cargo's network. The SAF is derived from used cooking oil and food waste, delivering approximately 80% lower lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions than conventional jet fuel. Lifecycle emissions reductions are estimated at 640,000 tonnes of CO₂e, with the full framework agreement potentially exceeding one million tonnes. The agreement supports DHL's target of at least 30% SAF blending across all air transport by 2030 and IAG's target of 10% SAF usage by the same year.
Source: ESG Today / DHL / IAG Cargo (15 April 2026)
Context: The "cooking oil jet fuel" framing understates the supply chain complexity here: used cooking oil (UCO) is collected from restaurants, food manufacturers, and households across multiple countries, refined and processed into HEFA-SPK (Hydroprocessed Esters and Fatty Acids — Synthesised Paraffinic Kerosene), then blended at approved ratios at Heathrow. The 640,000 tonnes CO₂e reduction is equivalent to removing roughly 140,000 cars from UK roads for a year. The structural significance: DHL and IAG are pooling volumes to achieve the scale needed for cost-competitive SAF procurement — a replication of the Singapore SAF levy model in commercial rather than regulatory form. For Indian aviation and logistics companies: Indigo, Air India, and Indian express cargo operators using European routes are now downstream of this deal structure; SAF pricing and availability on trans-European routes will tighten as demand grows. India's own used cooking oil collection infrastructure — currently minimal — is a future supply source worth developing now.
Circular Economy
Rivian and Redwood Materials Deploy Second-Life EV Batteries as Grid Storage at Illinois Manufacturing Plant
Rivian and Redwood Materials launched a circular economy project on 15 April to deploy energy storage systems using second-life EV battery packs at Rivian's manufacturing facility in Normal, Illinois. The initial installation utilises over 100 second-life Rivian battery packs, delivering 10 MWh of dispatchable on-site energy storage. The project demonstrates how EV batteries — which typically retain 70–80% of original capacity when removed from vehicles due to range anxiety or collision — can continue to provide grid services for 5–10 additional years before final recycling. Redwood Materials, founded by former Tesla CTO JB Straubel, has built a domestic battery recycling and refurbishment platform that processes batteries from consumer electronics, EVs, and now stationary storage. Straubel noted that "the massive amount of domestic battery assets already in the US market represents a strategic energy resource."
Source: ESG Today / Rivian / Redwood Materials (15 April 2026)
Context: The circular economy narrative here is economically compelling, not just environmentally: second-life batteries cost 30–50% less than new cells for stationary storage applications, enabling cheaper behind-the-meter storage for manufacturing facilities. Rivian's Normal plant consumes roughly 50–80 GWh of electricity annually — the 10 MWh pilot is small but serves as an operational proof point for a deployment that will scale. The strategic depth: Redwood processes Rivian's end-of-life batteries into cathode and anode materials that go back into new Rivian packs — closing the full loop. This is what genuine supply chain decarbonisation looks like: not purchasing offsets for battery waste, but redesigning the product lifecycle. For Indian EV manufacturers (Tata, Mahindra, Ola Electric): India's battery recycling ecosystem is nascent but building fast. The NITI Aayog's battery swap policy creates natural collection infrastructure for second-life programmes. The Rivian-Redwood model is 3–5 years ahead of where Indian OEMs currently sit, but the trajectory is the same.
Natural Capital
BTG Pactual TIG Raises $370 Million for Latin America Timberland Strategy, Targeting $1.5 Billion Over Five Years
BTG Pactual Timberland Investment Group (TIG) raised $370 million in initial commitments for its new core Latin America timberland strategy, targeting $1.5 billion in total investment over five years, focused on sustainably managed timberland across Chile, Uruguay, and Brazil. The fund targets institutional investors seeking inflation-resistant natural capital returns, with the firm citing the region's "unique biological and structural attributes" and rising demand for climate-aligned forestry assets. BTG Pactual TIG has decades of Latin American timberland experience and positions the fund to benefit from both timber revenue and carbon credit generation from sustainably managed forests. The raise reflects growing institutional appetite for natural capital as an asset class that delivers climate co-benefits alongside financial returns.
Source: ESG Today / BTG Pactual TIG (17 April 2026)
Context: Natural capital funds are the fastest-growing corner of sustainable finance: timberland, farmland, blue carbon, and ecosystem services are attracting institutional capital that can no longer find yield in conventional fixed income and is increasingly ESG-constrained. The $370 million initial close on a $1.5 billion target signals strong LP appetite — likely pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, and endowments seeking long-duration, inflation-linked cash flows. The Latin America geography is deliberate: Brazil, Chile, and Uruguay have well-established plantation forestry sectors with legal title certainty, unlike many tropical forest regions. The "core" strategy label is important: it implies lower risk, lower returns (~7–9%), and higher liquidity compared to value-add timberland funds — broadening the investor base to include more conservative allocators. For Indian pension funds (EPFO, NPS) and insurance companies: natural capital funds are not yet accessible at scale domestically, but the global template BTG is building will eventually find an Indian equivalent as SEBI's ESG fund regulations mature.

Carbon Markets
Microsoft Pauses All Carbon Removal Purchases: The Company That Bought 80% of the Market Just Stopped Buying
Microsoft staff informed carbon removal suppliers and partners in early April that the company is pausing future purchases of carbon removal credits — a decision first reported by Bloomberg on 11 April and confirmed across multiple outlets by 13 April. Microsoft's carbon removal programme has, by market estimates, accounted for approximately 80% of all contracted engineered carbon removal globally, making it the single largest source of voluntary carbon removal demand. The company has purchased hundreds of thousands of tonnes of removal credits annually — including BECCS, enhanced weathering, direct air capture, and biochar — from dozens of developers. A Microsoft spokesperson denied an indefinite halt, stating the company "continually reviews and assesses its carbon removal portfolio along with market conditions." The pause does not affect existing signed contracts but ends new dealmaking. Analysts at Carbon Pulse and MIT Technology Review described the pause as potentially triggering consolidation, project delays, and insolvencies across early-stage carbon removal developers.
Source: Bloomberg / Axios / Heatmap News / MIT Technology Review (11–13 April 2026)
Context: The voluntary carbon removal market is experiencing what economists call a "monopsony shock" — when the dominant buyer withdraws, the market reprices downward regardless of supply quality. Microsoft's AI buildout has dramatically increased its actual emissions (up 29% in FY2024), creating both the financial incentive to reduce carbon removal spending and the reputational need to justify any pause. The company is almost certainly renegotiating its portfolio strategy: the carbon removal market has matured enough that prices are rising (DAC credits now trade at $300–$500/tonne; BECCS at $150–250/tonne), and Microsoft may be seeking to restructure toward higher-quality, lower-cost options rather than stopping entirely. For the CDR ecosystem: the companies most at risk are pre-commercial projects that had Microsoft LOIs but no signed contracts. The projects that survive will be those with diversified buyers, operational assets, and verified delivery track records. This is a painful but necessary market maturation event — the VCM was overconcentrated in a single anchor buyer, and that was always a structural fragility. India's nascent CDR sector — which has limited Microsoft exposure — may paradoxically be better positioned as a result, as European corporate buyers seek diversified CDR supply.
Carbon Removal
Exomad Green and Supercritical Sign 500,000-Tonne Biochar Carbon Removal Deal — The Bolivian Startup Defying Microsoft's Shadow
Exomad Green and Supercritical signed a three-year agreement on 16 April for the purchase of up to 500,000 tonnes of biochar carbon removal (BCR) credits, covering Exomad's remaining 2026 inventory plus allocations for 2027 and 2028. Exomad Green converts forestry waste from Bolivia's sustainably managed plantations into biochar — a stable form of carbon that is mixed into agricultural soils, improving fertility while permanently sequestering carbon. The company has produced over 320,000 tonnes of verified biochar removal to date and expanded production by more than 200% year-over-year. Supercritical, a London-based corporate carbon removal marketplace, now accounts for approximately 35% of global corporate CDR purchases and has facilitated nearly two million tonnes across multiple removal pathways. The deal positions Exomad as the largest global biochar producer by delivered volume.
Source: ESG Today / Supercritical / Exomad Green (16 April 2026)
Context: The timing of this deal — three days after the Microsoft pause sent shockwaves through the VCM — is a pointed market signal. Exomad's 500,000-tonne deal with Supercritical demonstrates that corporate demand for CDR is not a Microsoft monopsony but a diversified buyer base. Supercritical's 35% market share represents hundreds of mid-sized corporate buyers (tech companies, financial services firms, professional services) each purchasing 1,000–50,000 tonnes annually — the kind of distributed demand base that insulates the market from single-buyer risk. Biochar's appeal in this context is its cost profile: at $50–150/tonne (significantly below DAC or BECCS), biochar removal is accessible to a wider range of corporate buyers who cannot afford premium engineered removal. The Bolivia geography is important: forestry waste feedstock from sustainably certified plantations provides co-benefits for local communities and biodiversity that premium corporate buyers now routinely require. For Indian businesses building voluntary carbon programmes: biochar from agricultural waste (rice husk, sugarcane bagasse, coconut shells) is a domestic production pathway that could supply Indian corporate buyers at cost structures competitive with anything in South America.
