EU Sustainability Reporting
European Commission Draft ESRS 2.0 Open for Public Consultation Through 3 June — 489 Mandatory Datapoints Removed (~60%+ Cut), All Voluntary Datapoints Removed, Flexible GHG Boundary, "Top-Down" Materiality; Double Materiality and 1.5°C Plan-Alignment Statement Retained
The European Commission on 6 May published its draft revised European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS 2.0) for a four-week public consultation closing 3 June 2026. Headline simplifications: removal of 489 previously mandatory datapoints (Commission states ~60%+ reduction in mandatory and ~70%+ reduction in total datapoints); removal of all voluntary datapoints; flexibility between operational and financial control as the GHG boundary; primary-microplastics-only disclosure; "top-down" materiality. Double materiality is retained. Climate transition plan disclosure persists, but companies must now explicitly state whether plans are 1.5°C-aligned. Applies to financial years starting 1 January 2027, with Wave 1 early adoption optional for 2026.
Source: Global Policy Watch / Covington / Latham & Watkins / EFRAG / European Commission (consultation closes 3 June 2026)
Why it matters for corporate ESG: A 60%+ cut in mandatory datapoints plus "top-down" materiality fundamentally changes the reporting-burden compass for in-scope CSRD filers. Double-materiality retention is the strategic anchor — Europe's distinctive architecture remains the global outlier — but the simplifications narrow the gap with IFRS S1/S2, which the IFRS-GRI alignment expansion in the same fortnight will deepen. For non-EU multinationals with EU branch operations (Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Alphabet, Meta, J&J, P&G, Coca-Cola, Toyota, Samsung, TCS, Infosys, Wipro, Reliance Europe, Tata Steel Europe, JSW Steel Europe) Article 40a remains operative but the disclosure burden is markedly lower. For BRSR Core in India, the ESRS 2.0 datapoint reductions remove the previous "ESRS is the harder standard, BRSR is the lighter one" framing — BRSR Core's quantitative granularity is now closer to ESRS than at any point since CSRD entered force.

SEC Climate-Rule Rescission
SEC Launches Formal 60-Day Notice-and-Comment Rescission of 2024 Corporate Climate Disclosure Rules — Chair Atkins Frames "Materiality" as Agency's "North Star," Cites Statutory-Authority Limits and Costs Outweighing Benefits
The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission on 30 May launched the formal notice-and-comment rescission of its 2024 climate-related disclosure rules, opening a 60-day public-comment window. The SEC's published rationale: the rules "exceed the scope of the agency's statutory authority" and impose "significant costs on companies and shareholders" that work against capital formation and public-company attractiveness. SEC Chair Paul Atkins: "SEC disclosure obligations should comply with statutory authority, be guided by materiality as the North Star, avoid dictating corporate behaviour, and be imposed only when benefits justify costs."

Why it matters for corporate ESG: The "exceeds statutory authority" framing is a post-Loper Bright argument that invalidates not just the 2024 rule but the statutory basis for any future SEC climate-disclosure rule under the current Court's administrative-law jurisprudence.
