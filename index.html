India

Duty waivers, drug shortages, and a carbon market that finally has teeth.
Regulatory & Policy "India's answer to a $9 billion supply shock? Zero duty and a new energy market playbook."
Trade Policy
India Slashes Customs Duty on 40 Petrochemical Products to Zero Amid West Asia Supply Crisis
The Ministry of Finance announced on 2 April a full customs duty exemption on 40 key petrochemical products — including polypropylene, polycarbonate, PVC, methanol, PTA, and polystyrene — effective immediately until 30 June 2026. The waiver, estimated to cost Rs 1,800 crore to the exchequer, benefits plastics, packaging, textiles, pharmaceuticals, automotive components, and chemical manufacturing. The government cited West Asia conflict disruptions, diverted domestic petrochemical output to LPG production, and surging input costs as rationale.
Source: Business Today / Business Standard / ANI (2–3 April 2026)
Context: This is crisis-mode industrial policy with ESG implications running both ways. On one hand, zero-duty petrochemical imports could temporarily increase India's fossil feedstock consumption. On the other, it prevents downstream manufacturing shutdowns that would cascade into job losses (the "S" in ESG). The real signal: India's petrochemical dependence on the Strait of Hormuz is now a Cabinet-level vulnerability. Watch for this temporary measure to accelerate the business case for bio-based and recycled alternatives — when virgin plastic gets this volatile, circular economy investments look like hedging, not charity.
Energy Markets
MNRE Approves 500 MW Contract-for-Difference Pilot to Transform India's Renewable Energy Market
The Ministry of New and Renewable Energy approved a pilot Contract for Difference (CfD) scheme on 30 March, marking India's shift from fixed Power Purchase Agreements to market-linked renewable energy pricing. SECI will tender 500 MW of RE capacity to supply 1,500 MWh during non-solar hours daily. A CfD Stabilization Fund of Rs 276 crore backs the programme. Developers will be selected via reverse bidding with a 125 MW cap per bidder, under 12-year build-own-operate contracts.
Source: SolarQuarter / Mercom India (30 March – 2 April 2026)
Context: This is a structural shift, not a subsidy tweak. CfDs — the mechanism that unlocked offshore wind in the UK — let RE generators sell on power exchanges while SECI settles the difference between strike price and market clearing price. For Indian RE developers, this means revenue upside when power prices spike (like now, during the energy crisis). The 125 MW cap per bidder ensures competition. Combined with the Hormuz-driven energy crisis, the timing makes CfDs less about policy experimentation and more about energy security infrastructure.
Large Company Actions & Big Moves "API costs up 300%. Freight up 20%. GDP growth down 0.5%. The invoice for Hormuz just arrived."
Pharma Crisis
Pharmexcil Urges Government to Allocate Key Chemicals as Drug Shortages Loom
The Pharmaceuticals Export Promotion Council (Pharmexcil) has flagged critically low inventories of propylene, methanol, ammonia, and butane — essential feedstocks for Active Pharmaceutical Ingredient (API) production. API costs have surged 200–300% in just 15 days due to West Asia supply disruptions. Chairman Namit Joshi warned that unless solvent manufacturers restore supplies quickly, India's pharmaceutical sector faces serious production disruptions. Key shipping routes — Red Sea, Strait of Hormuz, Gulf corridors — face rerouting risks, particularly for temperature-sensitive pharma products.
Source: Economic Times / ChemIndigest (1–3 April 2026)
Context: India supplies 20% of the world's generic medicines. A pharma supply chain breakdown here isn't just an Indian problem — it's a global health security crisis. Three ESG threads converge: (1) the "S" dimension — drug access for billions depends on Indian API production; (2) the "G" dimension — Pharmexcil is effectively asking for wartime-style chemical allocation, a governance stress test; (3) the business resilience angle — companies with diversified sourcing and domestic API capacity will emerge as the new "safe" pharma investments. The Rs 1,800 crore duty waiver (above) is the government's first response.
Macro Impact
West Asia War Shockwaves Ripple Through India's Economy: GDP Growth Cut 0.5%, CAD Widens $9–10 Billion
India's 88% crude oil import dependency and 90% LPG import transit through the Strait of Hormuz is now hitting the P&L. Analysts project GDP growth reduced by 0.5 percentage points, current account deficit widened by $9–10 billion (0.4% of GDP), and CPI inflation pushed to 4.5%. Medical device plastics are up 50%, piped natural gas has nearly doubled, container freight rates surged 15–20%, and war-risk insurance premiums have skyrocketed. Pharma exporters brace for a Rs 5,000-crore hit from freight costs alone.
Source: Business Today / Outlook Business / The Wire (3 April 2026)
Context: This is the comprehensive damage assessment Indian boardrooms are reading right now. Every number here feeds into ESG risk models: higher energy costs increase Scope 1 & 2 emissions intensity (as companies fall back on diesel), supply chain disruptions test Scope 3 reporting accuracy, and inflationary pressure on lower-income populations is a material social risk. For ESG investors: Indian companies with hedged energy positions, diversified supply chains, and domestic manufacturing are the resilience plays. The rest just got repriced.

Regulatory & Policy "Japan launched a carbon market. Korea finalised ISSB reporting. The EU can't decide if its deforestation law should exist."
Carbon Pricing
Japan Launches Mandatory GX-ETS Carbon Pricing: 300–400 Companies, 60% of National Emissions Covered
Japan's Green Transformation Emissions Trading Scheme (GX-ETS) became mandatory on 1 April 2026, transitioning from a voluntary phase that ran through 2025. The scheme covers Scope 1 emissions for 300–400 companies exceeding 100,000 tCO₂e annually — roughly 60% of Japan's total CO₂ emissions. Companies receive annual emission allowances; those exceeding limits must purchase surplus allowances from others. Price floor and ceiling mechanisms ensure predictability. Up to 10% of compliance obligations can be met with J-Credits and Joint Crediting Mechanism (JCM) credits.
Source: MSCI / ICAP / Carbon Pulse (1 April 2026)
Context: Asia's carbon pricing dominoes are falling fast. Japan's GX-ETS is now the world's newest major compliance carbon market, joining the EU ETS, UK ETS, and China's national scheme. For Indian exporters: Japan is India's 4th-largest trading partner. Japanese buyers will increasingly factor embedded carbon costs into procurement decisions. With India's own CCTS going live simultaneously, bilateral carbon credit recognition (via JCM) could become a trade facilitation tool. The GX-ETS also backstops Japan's ¥20 trillion GX Transition Bond programme — the world's largest sovereign transition finance instrument.
Reporting
South Korea Finalises ISSB-Aligned Mandatory Sustainability Reporting from 2028
South Korea's Financial Services Commission released its draft roadmap for mandatory sustainability disclosures: KOSPI-listed companies with assets exceeding KRW 30 trillion ($20.4 billion) must report from 2028 based on 2027 data. Companies above KRW 10 trillion ($6.8 billion) follow one year later. The Korean Sustainability Standards Board (KSSB) standards align broadly with ISSB's IFRS S1 and S2. Third-party assurance starts as optional, with mandatory adoption phased in gradually. Public consultation closes April 2026, with final roadmap expected imminently.
Source: ESG Today / Carbon Credits / Seoul Economic Daily (April 2026)
Context: Korea joins Japan, the EU, and Singapore in mandating ISSB-aligned reporting — building the interoperable global baseline the ISSB was designed to create. The KRW 30 trillion threshold means Samsung, SK Hynix, Hyundai, and POSCO are first in line. For Indian companies in Korean supply chains (auto parts, steel, chemicals): your sustainability data quality just became a procurement prerequisite. India's BRSR framework, while comprehensive, is not yet ISSB-aligned — this gap will increasingly matter for cross-border capital flows.
EU Regulation
EU Deforestation Law: Simplification Review Due April 30 as EUDR Stalls Again
The EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) — already delayed twice — now applies from 30 December 2026 for large operators and 30 June 2027 for small enterprises. By 30 April 2026, the European Commission must deliver a simplification review assessing administrative burden, which "should, where appropriate, be accompanied by a legislative proposal" — meaning the rules could change yet again before taking effect. Printed products have been removed from scope. Micro and small operators will submit one-off simplified declarations.
Source: European Parliament / EU Council (April 2026)
Context: The EUDR is becoming the EU's most politically fraught sustainability regulation — delayed, simplified, and potentially watered down before it even applies. But Indian agricultural exporters shouldn't relax: the April 30 review could go either way, and major commodity traders are already building traceability systems regardless. Coffee, cocoa, soy, palm oil, rubber, timber, and cattle product exporters to the EU should treat December 2026 as a hard deadline. The risk isn't the regulation changing — it's being unprepared when it finally lands.

Climate Finance
Green Climate Fund Approves $960 Million, Portfolio Tops $20 Billion Across 354 Projects
The Green Climate Fund approved $960.3 million in new financing at its 44th Board meeting — 18 projects spanning mitigation, adaptation, and resilience. Africa received nearly half ($441 million), including the $250 million ASCENT-GREEN programme with the World Bank supporting resilient energy access across 21 Eastern and Southern African countries — the single largest project in this round. New regional offices will open in Panama City, Amman, Nairobi, Abidjan, and Suva. Total portfolio: 354 projects across developing economies.
Source: ESG News / GCF / Sustainability Online (April 2026)
Context: The GCF crossing $20 billion is a milestone, but context matters: the US withdrew from the Fund in January 2025, leaving a $3 billion funding gap. That the Fund is still scaling despite this shows multilateral climate finance has decoupled from US federal policy. For India: GCF has approved $1.4 billion for Indian projects to date, and the new regional office structure could streamline access. The ASCENT-GREEN programme's focus on energy access in Africa aligns with India's own South-South cooperation narrative — potential for co-financing and technology transfer.
Energy Sovereignty
France Launches 12 GW Renewable Tender with Anti-China Supply Chain Rules
France announced tenders for 12 GW of renewable energy on 2 April: 10 GW across seven offshore wind projects, 1.2 GW solar, and 0.8 GW onshore wind. New "resilience criteria" cap Chinese components at 4 out of 9 strategic parts per project. The tenders, two years late due to political gridlock over financing, support France's 10-year energy law target of 15 GW offshore wind by 2035 (up from under 2 GW today). Results expected by late 2026 or early 2027.
Source: ESG News / Reuters / Offshore Wind (2–3 April 2026)
Context: France just made "energy sovereignty" tangible — 12 GW with explicit supply chain localization rules. The 4-out-of-9 Chinese component cap is the most concrete anti-dependency measure any EU country has enacted for renewables. For India: this is validation of India's own PLI scheme for solar modules (domestic manufacturing capacity hit 144 GW in 2025). If European tenders increasingly restrict Chinese components, Indian manufacturers — already the third-largest solar module producers — could fill the gap. The Hormuz crisis makes the energy sovereignty argument irresistible.
Carbon Markets
EU Proposes Carbon Market Concessions as Energy-Intensive Industries Face 'Perfect Storm'
Ten EU member states called on the European Commission to extend free carbon permit allocation to industrial sectors beyond 2034, as energy costs soar from the Middle East conflict and US tariffs constrain export competitiveness. Commission President von der Leyen pledged near-term ETS revisions, with a comprehensive review planned for July 2026. A new proposal would stop invalidation of allowances in the Market Stability Reserve, keeping them as a buffer against price spikes. EU carbon prices have hit 11-month lows below €68/tonne amid uncertainty.
Source: Energy Connects / ESG Today / Carbon Herald (April 2026)
Context: The EU is walking back its own climate ambition under industrial pressure — a politically rational but climatically dangerous move. Free carbon allowances are a subsidy to polluters that CBAM was designed to replace. Extending them beyond 2034 would undermine CBAM's rationale and send a signal that carbon pricing buckles under economic stress. For Indian exporters: if CBAM weakens, the urgency to decarbonise for EU market access decreases. But the smarter bet is to stay ahead — carbon costs are going up globally (see Japan, India, Korea this week), and companies that decarbonise now lock in long-term competitive advantage.
Fashion
H&M and Stella McCartney Launch Sustainability Insights Board in London
H&M and designer Stella McCartney convened the first meeting of their Sustainability Insights Board in London this week, bringing together technologist Kiara Nirghin, model Amelia Gray, fashion editor Susie Lau, activist Adwoa Aboah, and singer Anitta alongside McCartney and H&M sustainability experts. The board will focus on materials innovation, circularity, animal welfare, and transparent communication about sustainability performance. Fashion strategist Julie Gilhart moderates the conversations.
Source: H&M Group / ESG News / WWD (April 2026)
Context: H&M's move is governance innovation disguised as PR — creating a named, external accountability mechanism for sustainability commitments. For the world's second-largest fashion retailer (after Inditex), this matters because fashion accounts for 10% of global carbon emissions and is under intense EU regulatory scrutiny (CSRD, textile waste directives, potential EU digital product passports). The board composition — mixing culture, technology, and activism — signals H&M is trying to stay ahead of consumer sentiment, not just regulation. India's $40 billion textile export sector should watch: sustainability governance is becoming a sourcing criterion.

E-Fuels
Japan's ENEOS Produces Carbon-Neutral Fuel From Air — Then Pauses Scale-Up Over Economics
ENEOS Corporation successfully produced synthetic fuel (e-fuel) at its Yokohama demonstration facility using only atmospheric CO₂, water, and renewable electricity — a first for integrated e-fuel production at scale. The facility, operational since September 2024, produces approximately one barrel per day (159 litres). The fuel works in existing combustion engines, aircraft, and ships without modification, and was demonstrated in vehicles and planned for Osaka Expo shuttle buses. However, ENEOS has paused large-scale commercialisation plans, citing economic constraints that make the fuel uncompetitive at current prices.
Source: Times of India / The Defense News (April 2026)
Context: The technology works. The economics don't — yet. But two things have changed since ENEOS paused: (1) the Hormuz crisis has spiked fossil fuel prices, shrinking the cost gap for e-fuels; (2) Japan's GX-ETS (launched this week) now prices carbon, making fossil alternatives relatively more expensive. E-fuels matter for "hard-to-electrify" sectors — aviation, shipping, heavy machinery — where batteries can't reach. For India's aviation sector (projected to be the 3rd-largest by 2030), e-fuel technology represents a potential decarbonisation pathway that doesn't require fleet replacement. Watch for the economics to flip as carbon pricing spreads.
Circular Economy
23-Year-Old Solar Modules Still Deliver 88% Power: Brazil Study Validates Second-Life Reuse
Researchers at Brazil's Federal University of Santa Catarina (UFSC) tested 76 multicrystalline PV modules recovered from a decommissioned off-grid system on Ratones Island that had operated for over 22 years. Results: 68% of modules were approved for second-life use, retaining 87–88% of original power output with annual degradation of just 0.4–0.7%. Even repaired units with minor cracks showed only mild hotspots posing no immediate risk. Lead researcher noted the strong technical case but acknowledged economic barriers: declining costs of new silicon PV make second-life economics challenging.
Source: PV Magazine (31 March 2026)
Context: By 2030, the world will face an estimated 8 million tonnes of solar panel waste annually. This study proves reuse is technically viable — the bottleneck is economic, not engineering. India, which added 36.6 GW of solar in 2025 alone, will face a massive panel waste challenge within a decade. The 0.4–0.7% annual degradation rate means panels designed for 25 years could serve 35+ years in second-life applications — rural electrification, agricultural pumping, disaster relief. India's BIS standards and e-waste rules will need to accommodate second-life solar to prevent a circular economy opportunity from becoming a landfill crisis.
