India

Regulatory & Policy "India set its 2035 climate targets. Critics say the bar was already cleared."
NDC
India Approves 2035 Climate Targets: 47% Emissions Intensity Cut, 60% Non-Fossil Power Capacity
India's cabinet approved its updated NDC under the Paris Agreement on 25 March: reduce emissions intensity of GDP by 47% (vs 2005), achieve 60% non-fossil installed electricity capacity, and create a carbon sink of 3.5–4.0 billion tonnes CO₂e through forest cover by 2035. India has already reduced emissions intensity by 36% between 2005–2020 and surpassed 52% non-fossil capacity by early 2026 — ahead of schedule.
Source: ESG Today / WRI / Down To Earth (25–27 March 2026)
Context: Climate Action Tracker called the targets underwhelming — the 47% intensity cut is marginal improvement over the 2030 target of 45%, and India already tracks toward 60% non-fossil capacity under current policies. The critical gap: intensity targets allow absolute emissions to rise alongside GDP growth. India, the world's third-largest emitter at ~6.5% of global GHGs, has chosen incrementalism. Watch whether carbon market infrastructure (launched at Prakriti 2026) and the ₹20,000 crore CCUS allocation translate into measurable abatement.
Waste
20.7 Million Tonnes of Plastic Packaging Waste Recycled Under EPR Rules Since 2022
Union Minister Bhupender Yadav told Parliament on 23 March that approximately 20.7 million tonnes of plastic packaging waste have been recycled since EPR Guidelines came into force in 2022. Some 60,128 producers, importers, and brand owners are registered, alongside 3,012 plastic waste processors on the Centralized Online EPR Portal. Environmental compensation of Rs 48.42 crore imposed, Rs 47.35 crore recovered (2018–2024).
Source: Down To Earth (24 March 2026)
Context: India's EPR framework is generating real tonnage — 20.7 million tonnes is serious. But CPCB issued show-cause notices in November 2024, December 2024, and March 2026 to entities missing targets. With the Iran conflict now spiking virgin plastic prices, recycled plastic economics could improve dramatically — turning regulatory compliance into competitive advantage for early movers.
Capital Markets
SEBI Eases Trade Settlement Rules for Foreign Investors Amid Record Outflows
SEBI eased settlement rules on 23 March, allowing large foreign investors to settle trades on a net basis instead of settling each transaction separately. The move reduces funding requirements and trading costs amid the heaviest FII outflows in two decades. Net settlement becomes mandatory from 31 December 2026 and benefits exchange-traded and index funds on rebalancing days.
Source: Reuters / Bloomberg (23 March 2026)
Context: This is governance ("G") meeting market reality. With FIIs pulling out at record pace — partly driven by geopolitical risk from the Iran conflict — SEBI is lowering operational friction to retain foreign capital. The timing, amid $12B+ HDFC Bank governance fallout (reported last week), signals SEBI is in active damage-control mode.
Large Company Actions & Energy "India sent fuel to Sri Lanka. The real story? Two days of LPG reserves."
Geopolitics
India Sends 38,000 Metric Tonnes of Fuel to Sri Lanka Amid Gulf Supply Disruptions
India shipped 38,000 MT of petroleum — 20,000 MT diesel and 18,000 MT petrol — to Sri Lanka on 28 March, following a Modi–Dissanayake phone call on 24 March. Lanka IOC's pre-arranged supplies from Middle East and Singapore suppliers were disrupted after they invoked force majeure, citing vessel unavailability linked to the Iran conflict. Jaishankar and Sri Lanka's Foreign Minister Herath also coordinated on 23 March.
Source: The Hindu / Business Today (28–29 March 2026)
Context: This is India's "Neighbourhood First" policy stress-tested in real time. Sri Lanka's force majeure moment illustrates how the Iran conflict creates cascading energy insecurity across South Asia. India, importing ~85% of its crude itself, stepped up as regional energy guarantor. As fossil fuel supply chains fracture, India's strategic petroleum reserve and refining capacity becomes a geopolitical "S" (social stability) asset.
Energy Security
When Supply Chains Break, Waste Can Fill the Gap
With ~90% of India's LPG imports transiting the Strait of Hormuz and only two days of LPG reserves (vs 60+ days of oil), the article argues India's waste-to-energy infrastructure could serve as a strategic buffer. The approaching Kharif sowing season adds urgency — urea and phosphate fertilisers depend on natural gas, also disrupted. The piece calls for extending backyard biogas subsidies expiring March 2026, and sanctioning capex for biogas and compost infrastructure.
Source: The Wire (March 2026)
Context: Two days of LPG reserves versus 60+ days of oil is a stunning vulnerability. Three ESG dots to connect: (1) energy security — waste-to-energy as strategic resilience, not just climate action; (2) food security — fertiliser supply chains running through war zones; (3) circular economy — biogas as both fuel substitute and agricultural input. The Hormuz crisis may be the forcing function India's waste-to-energy sector needed.
Carbon & Decarbonisation "Jindal Stainless just proved hard-to-abate doesn't mean impossible-to-abate."
Clean Energy
Jindal Stainless Commissions 315.6 MW Hybrid Wind-Solar Project to Abate 6.5 Lakh Tonnes CO₂/Year
Oyster Renewable Energy commissioned part capacity of a 315.6 MW solar-wind hybrid project for Jindal Stainless, spanning Madhya Pradesh (216 MW) and Gujarat (99 MW). Once fully operational, the project will abate ~6.5 lakh metric tonnes of carbon emissions per year. India's total installed power capacity crossed 524 GW in February 2026, with 7.79 GW solar added in early 2026 alone.
Source: PV Magazine India / Solar Quarter (27 March 2026)
Context: Jindal Stainless is a hard-to-abate industrial emitter — steel/stainless steel production is among the most carbon-intensive. A 315.6 MW hybrid RE project signals serious corporate decarbonisation, not greenwashing. At 6.5 lakh tonnes CO₂ abated annually, this delivers measurable Scope 2 reduction. The hybrid model — combining solar and wind for higher capacity utilisation — is increasingly the preferred structure for industrial offtakers.

Global

Regulatory & Policy "The WMO confirmed what we already feel. EFRAG is building voluntary on-ramps. The IEA says work from home."
Climate Science
UN Confirms Hottest Decade on Record: Earth 'Pushed Beyond Its Limits'
The WMO released its State of the Global Climate 2025 report on 23 March, confirming 2015–2025 as the hottest 11 years ever recorded. 2025 was the 2nd or 3rd hottest year at ~1.43°C above pre-industrial average. Ocean heat content hit record levels; warming rate over 2005–2025 doubled that of 1960–2005. Nearly 90% of oceans experienced at least one marine heatwave. Arctic sea ice averaged 10.10M km² — lowest in the satellite era. Sea level now ~11 cm higher than 1993.
Source: WMO / Earth.Org (23 March 2026)
Context: This is the scientific baseline against which every corporate net-zero pledge, every NDC (including India's cautious 2035 targets), and every ESG strategy must be measured. The ocean absorbing 18x annual human energy use is alarming — marine ecosystems are overloading. Physical climate risk is no longer a 2050 projection — it's in the P&L today via insurance costs, supply chain disruptions, and asset write-downs.
EU Reporting
EFRAG Launches Voluntary Sustainability Reporting Framework for Non-SME Companies Outside CSRD Scope
EFRAG launched a call for expressions of interest (deadline: 20 April 2026) targeting EU companies outside CSRD's mandatory scope — non-SMEs with fewer than 1,000 employees or turnover below €450M. The European Commission is expected to release a Voluntary Standard as a Delegated Act later in 2026, building on the VSME standard issued December 2024 and endorsed July 2025.
Source: EFRAG (23 March 2026)
Context: The Omnibus I Package narrowed CSRD's mandatory scope, but EFRAG is building the voluntary on-ramp. For Indian companies with European operations or supply chain relationships: even below the 1,000-employee threshold, voluntary reporting could become a competitive differentiator — or a de facto procurement requirement. "Voluntary" in EU regulatory language often precedes "mandatory" by 2–3 years.
IFRS
IFRS Foundation Proposes Sector-Specific Sustainability Standards for Agriculture & Utilities
The IFRS Foundation proposed sector-specific sustainability reporting guidance for agriculture and power/utilities on 26 March — standardising how high-impact industries report on climate risks, emissions, water use, and nature dependencies. The updates align with the broader ISSB agenda to make sustainability reporting decision-useful for investors.
Source: ESG Today (26 March 2026)
Context: Agriculture (~10% of global GHGs) and power are where ESG reporting is most consequential and least standardised. Sector-specific ISSB standards will create comparability that generic ESG ratings cannot — and will likely influence India's BRSR framework evolution. For Indian agri-exporters and power companies, early alignment is a strategic move before these become embedded in procurement and lending criteria.
Large Company Actions & Big Moves "Petrochemical margins jumped 4x. Germany committed €8 billion. The US paid $928M to kill wind power."
Supply Chain
Iran War Chokes Petrochemical Supply, Sends Plastic Prices Soaring to Four-Year Highs
The Strait of Hormuz closure disrupted ~1.2 million bpd of naphtha exports, tightening petrochemical feedstock. Asia's naphtha margin surged above $400/tonne over Brent (from ~$108 pre-conflict). Celanese raised prices across engineered materials; Dow plans polyethylene hikes; BASF and Wacker Chemie lifting prices; Lanxess hiked flame retardants by 35% and plasticizers by 50%. Rabobank estimates $20–25 billion of petrochemical products transit the Strait annually.
Source: Reuters (26 March 2026)
Context: When naphtha margins jump 4x, every downstream product — packaging, auto parts, textiles, medical supplies — gets repriced. Two ESG opportunities: (1) recycled plastics suddenly cost-competitive against virgin feedstock, supercharging circular economy business cases; (2) bio-based alternatives gain pricing advantage they've never had. India's 20.7M tonne EPR recycling achievement suddenly looks like strategic foresight.
Energy Crisis
IEA Urges Work From Home, Car Sharing, Reduced Flying to Ease Global Energy Crisis
Following Iran's disruption of oil supply through the Strait of Hormuz, the IEA issued 10 consumer recommendations: work from home, reduce highway speeds, increase public transport, promote car sharing, adopt efficient driving, reduce air travel. The IEA earlier coordinated a record 400-million-barrel strategic reserves release across 32 member nations — the largest deployment in history.
Source: Earth.Org / IEA (March 2026)
Context: The 400M barrel strategic reserve release signals severity. If work-from-home and reduced travel become crisis-normalised again, corporate Scope 3 (business travel, commuting) emissions could see a structural downshift. Companies with hybrid work infrastructure are better positioned. The crisis also strengthens the long-term investment case for EVs, public transit, and rail freight.
Climate Action
Germany Adopts €8 Billion Climate Action Plan: 67 Measures to Slash Emissions 65% by 2030
Germany adopted a comprehensive Climate Protection Program: €8B over four years, 67 measures across energy, transport, buildings, agriculture. Key: 12 GW additional onshore wind (preventing 6.5M tonnes CO₂), industrial electrification (saving 4.3M tonnes CO₂, reducing gas imports by 2.5B m³), €3B EV subsidy for 800,000 vehicles targeting lower-income buyers, and district heating expansion. Target: 65% emissions reduction vs 1990.
Source: ESG Today (26 March 2026)
Context: Germany explicitly linked decarbonisation to reducing "expensive and uncertain oil and gas" dependence — the Iran crisis making this visceral. The €3B EV subsidy targeting lower-income buyers addresses the social equity critique of green transition. For Indian exporters: Germany's industrial electrification push could create demand for Indian green hydrogen, solar components, and battery materials.
US Policy
US Pays TotalEnergies $928M in Taxpayer Money to Drop Offshore Wind, Reinvest in Oil & Gas
The US Interior Department on 23 March announced it will reimburse TotalEnergies $928 million for offshore wind leases off New York and North Carolina. In exchange, TotalEnergies will invest $928M in Rio Grande LNG (Texas) and Gulf oil/gas projects. Federal judges have dismissed similar national security justifications as "arbitrary and capricious." Separately, 163 environmental organisations demanded EPA Administrator Zeldin's firing.
Source: NPR / Earth.Org / Fortune (23 March 2026)
Context: Using $928M in public funds to pay an oil company to abandon renewables and reinvest in fossil fuels is unprecedented. This crystallises the US federal-state ESG divergence: Washington subsidises fossil fuel reversion while California, New York, and others forge ahead with climate disclosure laws. For global ESG investors, US climate policy is now a two-track system — company-level assessment matters more than ever.
Carbon & Decarbonisation "Wastewater became a carbon solution. KKR proved impact delivers 15x. The 'missing middle' got $450M."
Carbon Capture
Northern Lights Injects First Wastewater-Derived CO₂ Underground in Global First
Norway's Northern Lights project began storing biogenic CO₂ from wastewater treatment — a first. Captured emissions come from the Veas facility near Oslo (800,000+ residents). Carbon removal company Inherit has delivered CO₂ cargoes since February. Gas is liquefied, trucked to Øygarden, injected 2,600m below the seabed via a 100 km pipeline. Pilot: up to 7,000 tonnes/year; full system targeting 1.5M tonnes/year, expandable to 5M.
Source: Interesting Engineering (25 March 2026)
Context: Northern Lights expanded what "counts" for carbon capture. Wastewater plants are ubiquitous — every city has them, every one produces biogenic CO₂. If this scales, municipal waste infrastructure transforms from emission source to carbon removal network. For India with 1,600+ STPs (most underutilised), this opens a pathway to combine waste management and carbon removal — if economics and pipeline infrastructure follow.
Impact
KKR Impact Fund Earns 15x Return on CoolIT Sale to Ecolab for $4.75 Billion
Ecolab agreed to acquire Calgary-based CoolIT Systems from KKR for $4.75 billion — ~15x KKR's 2023 investment. CoolIT's liquid cooling for data centres consumes 30–40% less energy than air cooling while reducing water usage via closed-loop design. KKR's Kyle Matter: "We acted early on the conviction that rapid growth in computing demand would pose a significant challenge for data center efficiency." Ecolab will integrate CoolIT with its water management expertise.
Source: ESG Today (26 March 2026)
Context: 15x on an impact investment is a rebuttal to every LP who questioned whether impact can deliver alpha. KKR's Global Impact Strategy (est. 2018) targets SDGs 7, 9, and 13 — this exit proves the thesis. Data centres are projected to consume 8% of global electricity by 2030. Ecolab's $4.75B bet signals data centre sustainability infrastructure is now a mainstream industrial play, not niche cleantech.
Climate Tech
Climate Investment Raises $450M to Bridge the 'Missing Middle' in Climate Tech Finance
Climate Investment announced the final close of its $450M Decarbonization Acceleration Fund (DAF) on 25 March — its first Growth Equity vehicle targeting companies with validated technologies and commercial traction across heavy-emitting sectors. CI's Operational Value Add model has delivered $600M+ of operational value since 2019. The fund addresses the gap between early-stage venture and large-scale infrastructure capital.
Source: ESG Today / Climate Investment (25–27 March 2026)
Context: The "missing middle" is climate tech's deadliest valley: companies proven at pilot scale but unable to access the $50–200M needed for commercial scale-up. Too big for venture, too risky for infrastructure PE. Combined with KKR's 15x CoolIT exit, this week demonstrates climate tech investing is maturing into a full lifecycle of capital. For Indian hard-to-abate industries, funds like DAF represent scale-up capital that could bring global decarbonisation tech to Indian markets.
Taxonomy
Australia Strengthens $53.8 Billion Sustainable Debt Market with Taxonomy Guidance
ASFI released guidance on 24 March for issuing taxonomy-aligned use-of-proceeds debt — the first framework of its kind for Australia. Developed with Australian Treasury, sovereign debt managers, and New Zealand Treasury, it covers green, social, and sustainability bonds and loans. Australia issued $53.8B in sustainable debt in 2025, up 11% YoY despite weaker global markets.
Source: ESG News / ASFI (24 March 2026)
Context: Australia joins the EU, ASEAN, and India's carbon market infrastructure in building global plumbing for sustainable capital allocation. The $53.8B market growing 11% while global issuance consolidated at $800–900B (S&P) shows Asia-Pacific remains a growth engine. Cross-border taxonomy alignment between Australia, ASEAN, and India could unlock significant capital flows for climate projects.
