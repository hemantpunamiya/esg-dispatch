Carbon Market Rules
CERC Notifies Carbon Credit Certificate Trading Regulations 2026 Under CCTS — Floor & Forbearance Pricing, Power-Exchange Settlement, Grid Controller as Registry
The Central Electricity Regulatory Commission (CERC) notified the CERC (Terms and Conditions for Purchase and Sale of Carbon Credit Certificates) Regulations, 2026 — the operational rulebook for how Carbon Credit Certificates (CCCs) will be bought and sold under India's Carbon Credit Trading Scheme, 2023 (CCTS). CCCs will be traded primarily through recognised power exchanges; pricing will be market-driven within a regulatory band defined by floor and forbearance prices to prevent excessive volatility. The Bureau of Energy Efficiency is the scheme administrator; the Grid Controller of India serves as the registry, maintaining records of issuance, transfer, and holdings. Participating entities must register before trading and can only deal in volumes equivalent to their certified holdings, with strict penalties for non-compliance and potential suspension of up to six months for repeat defaulters. The first CCC trading window is expected by mid-2026, covering the nine sectors with notified GHG intensity targets — aluminium, chlor-alkali, cement, fertiliser, iron & steel, pulp & paper, petrochemicals, petroleum refineries, and textile (~16% of India's emissions).
Source: Energetica India / CERC notification / BEE / IETA Business Brief (May 2026)
Context: India's carbon market just moved from policy paper to operational infrastructure. The exchange-based settlement choice is the structurally important one. By piggy-backing CCC trading onto IEX and PXIL — the same platforms where India's electricity market and Renewable Energy Certificates already settle — CERC ensures liquidity, price transparency, and audit trails from day one rather than building a new bespoke exchange. The floor-forbearance price band borrows directly from the REC mechanism, which has worked in keeping trading orderly through demand cycles. For obligated entities (PAT-cohort plants in nine sectors): the next 12 months are about systems readiness — MRV under the new methodology, reconciliation with old PAT energy savings, and treasury planning for either purchase or sale of CCCs. For the voluntary market (Indian VCM developers, REIA, project sponsors): once the compliance market goes live, voluntary buyers will price-anchor against compliance forwards — expect REC-style yield curves to emerge by Q4 2026. For BRSR Principle 6: GHG-intensity disclosures from FY26 onwards will need to reconcile internal Scope 1 with sector-target overshoot or undershoot under CCTS, and Scope 2 emission factors are about to start adjusting as RECs interact with CCCs. For Indian banks (HDFC, ICICI, SBI): expect carbon-asset financing products — repo against CCCs, transition loans tied to compliance trajectory — to start appearing as Q3 2026 trading windows open.
Green Methanol Tender
SECI Releases Draft RfS for 500,000 TPA Green Methanol — 10-Year Contracts, 50,000–250,000 TPA Allocations, SIGHT-Subsidised Projects Excluded
India on 8 May released a draft request for selection (RfS) to procure 500,000 tonnes per annum of green hydrogen-based methanol production, designed to seed a domestic supply chain for green methanol — a key alternative fuel for industrial and shipping applications. The framework offers 10-year long-term contracts, with individual project allocations between 50,000 and 250,000 TPA. The Solar Energy Corporation of India (SECI), under the Ministry of New and Renewable Energy, will manage the process. Projects already subsidised under the SIGHT programme for green hydrogen or ammonia are explicitly ineligible to prevent double-dipping; selected producers must deliver contracted volumes and bear all production and infrastructure costs. The tender follows India's 2025 green ammonia auction (~724,000 TPA contracted across 11 signed agreements) and complements the National Green Hydrogen Mission target of 5 million tonnes per annum of green hydrogen production by 2030.
Source: Channel I Am / SECI draft RfS / Ministry of New and Renewable Energy (8 May 2026)
Context: Green methanol is the industrial-fuel pivot India needed to make. Green hydrogen and green ammonia together do not address two of India's largest hard-to-abate emission pools: shipping bunker fuel and downstream petrochemical feedstock. Methanol made from green hydrogen plus captured or biogenic CO₂ does both — it can be combusted directly in modified marine engines (the IMO carbon-intensity regulations from 2027 onwards make this commercially compelling), and it is the feedstock for the methanol-to-olefins (MTO) and methanol-to-aromatics (MTA) pathways that displace naphtha cracking. For Indian green hydrogen developers (Reliance, L&T Energy GreenTech, Adani New Industries, Avaada, ACME): methanol opens a second offtake route alongside ammonia, particularly where local CO₂ from cement or biogas plants makes the value chain shorter. For Indian shipping companies (Shipping Corporation of India, Great Eastern, Cochin Shipyard): the SECI tender is the first signal that domestic green-bunker supply could be price-competitive by 2028, removing a binding constraint on Indian-flagged vessels facing IMO compliance. The exclusion of SIGHT-subsidised projects is the policy guardrail that keeps each scheme honest — total subsidy stacking would inflate the LCOH artificially and break price discovery on the export-competitive curve.
Mobility Transition
Delhi Closes Public Consultation on Draft EV Policy 2026–2030 — New ICE 3-Wheelers Banned January 2027, ICE 2-Wheelers April 2028, ₹3,954 Crore Outlay
Today (10 May) is the closing day for stakeholder feedback on the Government of National Capital Territory of Delhi's Draft Electric Vehicle Policy 2026–2030, released on 11 April. The headline measures: a complete ban on new registrations of ICE three-wheelers from 1 January 2027, followed by a ban on new ICE two-wheeler registrations from 1 April 2028, alongside ₹1 lakh scrappage incentives for EV uptake and 100% road tax exemption. The total fiscal outlay is ₹3,954.25 crore, comprising ₹1,236.25 crore for purchase incentives, ₹1,718 crore for scrappage, and ₹1,000 crore for charging infrastructure. The policy is in force until 31 March 2030. Two-wheelers up to ₹2.25 lakh receive ₹10,000/kWh in year 1, declining annually. The Society of Indian Automobile Manufacturers has publicly opposed the bans-first framing, arguing that enabling infrastructure should precede mandates.
Source: BikeWale / Mondaq / Goodreturns / Delhi government draft policy (4 April – 10 May 2026)
Context: Delhi is testing whether a state-level fossil-fuel registration ban can drive policy precedent the way California's ZEV mandate has shaped US auto-emissions trajectory for two decades. CRISIL projects ~600,000 electric two-wheeler additions in Delhi alone over the policy window — a number large enough to create a viable secondary battery supply chain in the NCR. The 1 January 2027 three-wheeler date is critical: Delhi's three-wheeler fleet is roughly 100,000 vehicles, dominated by Bajaj, Mahindra Electric (Treo), Piaggio, and Atul Auto, and conversion economics already favour electric. For two-wheeler OEMs (Hero MotoCorp, Bajaj, TVS, Honda Motorcycle & Scooter India, Royal Enfield): April 2028 is roughly two product cycles away, but the policy's effective date demands serious capex commitments by Q1 2027 — Delhi is one of India's largest 2W markets. For component suppliers (battery makers, motor manufacturers, charging-infrastructure companies including Ather Grid, Tata Power, Statiq): the Delhi outlay alone is bigger than most state-level EV programmes combined and creates demand-side certainty. For BRSR Scope 3 in auto and logistics: Delhi-registered fleets in 2027–2030 will need to start factoring forward-looking ICE retirement risk into their disclosed transition plans, particularly any company with a captive last-mile logistics arm in Delhi-NCR.

Q4 FY26 Results
SBI Q4 FY26: Net Profit Up 5.6% to ₹19,684 Crore But Shares Crash 7% on NII Miss — Dividend Declared at ₹17.35
State Bank of India on 8 May reported a Q4 FY26 net profit of ₹19,683.75 crore, up 5.5% YoY — its highest-ever quarterly figure — but shares fell over 7% intra-day after net interest income (NII) growth came in below Street expectations and asset quality improvements failed to offset margin compression. The board declared a dividend of ₹17.35 per share. Beyond the headline number, SBI's Sustainability Report 2024–25 (released alongside the annual report) reaffirmed the bank's commitment to becoming net-zero by 2055, with a goal of having at least 7.5% of its loan book classified as "green" by 2030. SBI's ESG & Climate Finance Unit, established in 2024, now anchors green-bond issuance, transition-loan structuring, and climate-risk integration into the credit underwriting process — areas where Indian banks have lagged peer Asian institutions. Total green-portfolio disclosures will be a material disclosure under BRSR Core Principle 6 from FY26 reporting onwards.
Source: Business Standard / Groww / SBI Sustainability Report 2024–25 (8 May 2026)
Context: The market reaction to SBI is the more analytically interesting half of the story. Indian bank investors no longer reward record headline profits when underlying NIM and NII signal margin pressure — and SBI's relatively muted green-portfolio progress (still well under the 7.5% target with four FY years remaining) is exactly the kind of disclosure that will increasingly differentiate Indian PSU banks from private peers in ESG-screened debt and equity flows. For India's other large lenders (HDFC, ICICI, Axis, Bank of Baroda — the latter posted Q4 FY26 net profit up 11% to ₹5,616 crore on the same day): SBI's combined "record profit + ESG progress trajectory" framing is becoming the template for how Indian bank quarterly results will be priced. For corporate borrowers seeking green or transition-tagged debt: HDFC has its $300 million sustainable finance bond framework, ICICI runs a large green-bond programme, but SBI's scale (~₹54 lakh crore advances book) makes its 7.5% green-loan target the single largest visible commitment in Indian banking. For BRSR Principle 6 preparers in the financial sector: SBI's disclosed green-loan classification methodology is now the de facto industry baseline that auditors will benchmark against. For climate-risk supervisors (RBI's incipient framework): the SBI Sustainability Report sets the granularity floor for what large public-sector banks should disclose under any future RBI climate-risk circular.

Green Hydrogen Export
DPA Kandla & GH2 Solar Sign MoU for India's First Liquid Green Hydrogen Export Hub — Joint Feasibility Study to Cover Liquefaction, Cryogenic Storage, Marine Export
The Deendayal Port Authority (DPA), Kandla, and GH2 Solar Limited on 5 May signed a Memorandum of Understanding to undertake a "Joint Feasibility Study and Strategic Roadmap" for the export of Liquid Green Hydrogen (LH₂) from Kandla Port, Gujarat. The MoU positions Kandla as India's first designated Green Hydrogen Export Hub and authorises a comprehensive technical, regulatory, and commercial assessment of the end-to-end infrastructure required — liquefaction units, cryogenic storage terminals, pipeline connectivity, and berthing facilities for LH₂-class carriers — to enable large-scale exports to international markets. GH2 Solar is an integrated green-hydrogen platform spanning solar generation, electrolyser manufacturing, and green-hydrogen production. DPA Kandla brings high cargo-handling capacity and existing bulk-liquid terminal infrastructure. The collaboration is aligned with India's National Green Hydrogen Mission target of 5 million tonnes per annum of green hydrogen production by 2030, with a portion explicitly earmarked for export.
Source: ANI / Energetica India / Fuel Cells Works / India Shipping News (5–8 May 2026)
Context: Kandla is the structurally correct port choice and the timing is the analytically interesting part. Kandla is India's deepest west-coast bulk-liquid port already handling LPG, LNG, and crude — meaning the cryogenic, vapour-recovery, and ship-vetting expertise required for LH₂ exists in nascent form, unlike Mundra or Hazira which would need to build from scratch. Liquid hydrogen at -253°C is materially harder to handle than LNG at -162°C; even a feasibility study at Kandla means India is committing to the full LH₂ technology stack rather than the easier ammonia-export workaround that Reliance, L&T Energy GreenTech, and Avaada have been pursuing. For European hydrogen importers (Germany's H2Global, Netherlands' HyNetwork, Spain's H2Med corridor): Kandla provides the first credible Indian LH₂ source against which the existing Australian (Yara, Origin) and Saudi (NEOM Green Hydrogen Project) export propositions can be benchmarked on cost. For Indian shipbuilders (Cochin Shipyard, L&T Shipbuilding): LH₂ carrier orders represent a multi-billion-dollar order book opportunity, with Korean and Japanese yards currently leading. For competing Indian ports (JNPA, Mundra, Paradip, Vizag): the Kandla announcement sets the de facto standard — others must announce their own LH₂ feasibility studies within 6–9 months or accept losing the strategic positioning. For DPA Kandla's parent ministry: LH₂ infrastructure now becomes a national-priority capex case rather than a port-specific one.

SEC Climate Rule
SEC Begins Formal Process to Rescind 2024 Climate Disclosure Rule — OMB Filing on 7 May, Chairman's Direction, Court Order Forces Decision
The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission on 7 May submitted a filing to the U.S. Office of Management and Budget formally beginning the process of rescinding the 2024 climate disclosure rules — the regulation that would have required public companies to disclose climate-related risks, mitigation plans, severe-weather financial impacts, and Scope 1/2 (and certain Scope 3) greenhouse gas emissions. An SEC spokesperson confirmed: "At the Chairman's direction, SEC staff is preparing a recommendation to the Commission to rescind the agency's 2024 climate rules." The move follows a September 2025 U.S. Court of Appeals order directing the SEC to determine the rule's fate after the agency declined to defend it. SEC Chair Paul Atkins framed the decision around "returning the agency to its core mandate – in line with its legal authority – restoring a materiality-focused approach to securities regulation." The full rescission process is expected to require proposed rules, public comments, and likely litigation, taking several months at minimum.
Source: ESG Today / SEC / U.S. Court of Appeals / U.S. Office of Management and Budget (7 May 2026)
Context: This formalises what has been operational reality for over a year. The 2024 climate rule has not been enforced since the SEC dropped its legal defence in March 2025; the OMB filing is the procedural step that converts non-enforcement into formal repeal. The interesting question is what fills the vacuum. California's SB 253 and SB 261 still require Scope 1/2 (and 3, from 2027) disclosure for companies operating in the state with over $1 billion in revenue, with first reporting obligations beginning in 2026 — meaning roughly the same large-cap S&P 500 cohort that would have been captured under the SEC rule will still need to disclose against California's framework. New York, Washington, Illinois, and New Jersey have similar legislative drafts at varying stages. For multinationals with EU operations: CSRD (now post-omnibus) still applies. For Indian companies listed in the US (Infosys, Wipro, ICICI Bank, Wipro ADR, HDFC ADR, Tata Motors): the SEC repeal removes a US-domicile climate disclosure obligation but does nothing to relieve the same companies' BRSR Core requirements at home, where third-party assurance becomes mandatory for the top 500 companies in FY26. Net effect: US capital markets are abandoning federal climate disclosure consensus, but state-level and international convergence (CSRD, ISSB, BRSR, California SB 253) means most large-cap multinationals must still disclose against multiple frameworks — only the federal SEC overlay is being removed. Watch for an SEC voluntary "principles-based" framework as a replacement, which would substantially lower comparability versus mandatory disclosure.
EU Sustainability Reporting
European Commission Opens Consultation on Revised ESRS — Mandatory Data Points Cut by Over 60%, Total Cut by Over 70%, ~30%+ Cost Reduction; Voluntary Standard for ≤1,000 Employees
The European Commission on 6 May opened a four-week public consultation on the revised European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) and a separate voluntary Sustainability Reporting Standard for undertakings with up to 1,000 employees that fall outside the mandatory CSRD scope. The revised mandatory ESRS reduces required data points by over 60% and total data points by over 70%, alongside clearer guidance on double materiality and new flexibilities on undue cost or effort. Sector-specific ESRS standards have been discontinued. Overall reporting costs per company are expected to fall by more than 30%. The voluntary standard is designed to give SMEs and mid-caps a proportionate framework when value-chain partners ask for sustainability data. Stakeholder feedback closes 3 June 2026; the revised ESRS are expected to apply from FY 2027, with possible voluntary early-adoption from FY 2026 subject to timely Commission adoption and member-state implementation of the CSRD into national law.
Source: European Commission / EFRAG / EU CSRD documentation (6 May 2026)
Context: This is the operational follow-through to February 2026's Omnibus directive, which narrowed CSRD scope to entities with over 1,000 employees and €450 million net turnover. The ESRS consultation reduces what those remaining in-scope companies actually have to disclose — from approximately 1,073 mandatory data points down to roughly 320, with sector-specific standards entirely removed. For European corporates that had already invested heavily in ESRS readiness in 2024–25 (Siemens, Volkswagen, ENI, TotalEnergies, Allianz, Maersk, Stellantis): much of that investment now exceeds requirements, but the underlying sustainability data infrastructure stays useful for ISSB, GRI, and SBTi disclosure. For Indian listed companies with European operations or supply chains (Tata Steel, Mahindra, Wipro, Infosys, Mahindra, Reliance Retail): scope-narrowing means many EU subsidiaries previously expected to file under CSRD now fall below the threshold — but the value-chain ripple still requires data submission to in-scope partners. For BRSR Core preparers in India: the EU's ESRS simplification is methodologically aligned with SEBI's mid-2025 ESG framework calibration (voluntary value-chain disclosure, phased third-party assurance), suggesting global ESG disclosure regimes are converging toward the same "less-but-better, with assurance" template. The 70% data-point cut is also an interesting signal of investor capacity: the EU has implicitly conceded that the original ESRS exceeded what investors were actually using.
ESG Investor Flows
ESG Shifting Tides — Harvard CGI Analysis Documents 14 Consecutive Months of ESG Fund Outflows, 12% Fund-Count Decline, "Sustainability" Mentions Below 2022 Levels
A Harvard Law School Forum on Corporate Governance analysis published 7 May, authored by Eleanor Viney, Neil McCarthy, and Emily Drazan Chapman of DragonGC, quantifies the retreat of the ESG framework from US corporate governance and capital markets through two lenses. ESG funds recorded a net outflow of $935 million in January 2026 — the fourteenth consecutive month of negative flows. The number of US ESG-labelled funds fell from 835 to 735 over twelve months — a 12% contraction; broad-ESG-focus funds lost 69 funds (-17%). On the disclosure side, "sustainability" mentions in S&P 500 DEF 14A proxy statements declined for the first time in 2025, dropping by 289 mentions (S&P 500) and 413 mentions (Fortune 1000); preliminary 2026 data show the decline accelerating below 2022 levels. Notably, Form 10-K risk-factor disclosures of "sustainability" continued increasing — meaning companies are still reporting material climate risk, just no longer using ESG vocabulary in voluntary investor-facing communications. Target Corporation eliminated 100% of explicit ESG mentions between 2023 and 2025, reframing language toward "business resiliency."
Source: Harvard Law School Forum on Corporate Governance / DragonGC (7 May 2026)
Context: The Harvard analysis frames the contraction as primarily a re-branding event rather than an abandonment of underlying activity — and that distinction is the actionable insight. ESG-focused funds are losing assets, ESG terminology is being eliminated from proxy statements, but Form 10-K disclosure of climate risk is still rising. The story is that corporate America is decoupling sustainability disclosure from ESG branding for political-risk-management reasons, not because sustainability disclosure itself is becoming less material. The drivers cited — "unconstitutional vagueness" doctrine in pending 2025–2026 ESG litigation, the SEC climate-rule rollback, Republican-led state attorneys-general anti-ESG enforcement — are all jurisdiction-specific to the US. For European, Asian, and Indian markets: the rebranding pressure is essentially absent. ESG language remains current in CSRD, ISSB, BRSR, and APAC sustainable-finance contexts. For US asset managers managing global ESG-tilt funds (BlackRock, State Street, Vanguard, Capital Group): the rebrand-versus-rebuild question is now a real product strategy decision — keep "ESG" in fund names and lose AUM, or rebrand and rebuild against expanded "sustainable" or "transition" terminology. For Indian and Asian corporates seeking ESG-tagged international debt or equity: the active demand pool is increasingly shifting away from ESG-labelled vehicles toward thematically-precise products (climate transition, biodiversity, just transition), which align better with use-of-proceeds disclosure than broad ESG screens. The political toxicity is a US phenomenon; the underlying climate-risk premium remains globally priced.

Sustainability Reporting
Grant Thornton 2025 ESG Report — 44% Emissions Reduction From 2019 Baseline, 90% Scope 1 & 2 Cut Targeted by 2030, First Multinational-Wide ESG Report
Grant Thornton on 6 May released its 2025 ESG Report, "Building with Intention" — its first ESG disclosure to consolidate progress across the entire multinational platform of 25,000 professionals across 18 firms in 150+ offices over 20 time zones. Headline numbers: a nearly 44% reduction in overall emissions from a 2019 baseline; renewable energy certificates covering 100% of US and India electricity consumption; SBTi-validated 2030 targets requiring a 90% reduction in Scope 1 and 2 emissions and a more than 50% reduction in Scope 3 emissions. Operationally, the firm reduced business travel, scaled remote-work patterns, and rolled out additional sustainability measures. Governance highlights include a 100% Corporate Equality Index score for the ninth consecutive year and Brandon Hall Group recognition for learning and development programmes.
Source: Grant Thornton "Building with Intention" 2025 ESG Report (6 May 2026)
Context: Grant Thornton is now setting the disclosure benchmark for global professional-services firms — its trajectory and granularity now exceed Big Four firms (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG) on multinational consolidation despite being smaller. The 44% trajectory matters because it implies a clear path to the 90% Scope 1 and 2 target by 2030 — Grant Thornton has executed roughly half of the required reduction in 6 of the 11 available years, well ahead of pace. For comparison: most professional-services SBTi commitments are Scope 1 and 2 reductions of 42–50% by 2030 against a 2019 baseline, so Grant Thornton's 90% target is materially more ambitious. The 100% RECs in US and India electricity consumption is the leverage — RECs are the cheapest tool to neutralise Scope 2, but the firm has chosen to use them as the foundation rather than as a make-good gesture, freeing engineering capex for harder Scope 1 abatement. For Indian competitors (PwC India, KPMG India, EY India, Deloitte India): the report sets the consolidation expectation that domestic Indian member firms must report individually in BRSR-Core-equivalent format AND consolidate into a global-network disclosure. For corporate clients seeking BRSR Core or CSRD support: a sustainability advisor that hasn't published a verified Scope 3 disclosure of its own is now in an awkward credibility spot — Grant Thornton's report is the new RFP-response baseline.

Copernicus C3S: April 2026 Joint Third-Warmest April on Record at 1.43°C Above Pre-Industrial — Sea Surface Temperatures Second-Highest, 46% of Global Ocean Under Marine Heatwave
The Copernicus Climate Change Service (C3S) on 8 May published its monthly bulletin for April 2026. Global surface air temperature reached 14.89°C — 0.52°C above the 1991–2020 average for April and 1.43°C above the estimated 1850–1900 pre-industrial reference. April 2026 is the joint third-warmest April in instrumental records, behind April 2024 and April 2025. The Copernicus Marine Service indicates April 2026 is on track to become the second-warmest April ever for the global ocean, with average sea surface temperature of 21.08°C — only narrowly below April 2024's record of 21.10°C. 46% of the global ocean experienced marine heatwave conditions during April — the third-largest extent on record, behind 2016 (48%) and 2024 (53%). Arctic sea ice fell to its second-lowest April extent. The European average for April was the tenth-warmest at 8.88°C (+0.50°C vs 1991–2020), with western and central Europe drier than average and southeastern Europe, the Caucasus, and the Maghreb wetter.
Source: Copernicus Climate Change Service (C3S) / Copernicus Marine Service / ERA5 dataset (8 May 2026)
Context: The April 2026 numbers continue the post-2023 elevated-baseline pattern — Earth has now spent 21 of the last 22 months above 1.5°C versus pre-industrial — but the more analytically interesting metric is the marine heatwave coverage. 46% of the global ocean simultaneously under heatwave conditions means the cumulative impact on coral reefs, fisheries, marine biodiversity, and ocean carbon uptake is now structural rather than episodic. For the Indian monsoon: the Bay of Bengal and Arabian Sea ran among the warmest sub-basins, supporting IMD's forecast of above-normal heatwave duration and warmer-than-normal monsoon onset. For global agricultural commodity markets (wheat, rice, sugar, palm oil, soybean): the 2026 ENSO transition (ENSO-neutral through April–June; 61% probability of El Niño emergence May–July) layered onto already-elevated SSTs raises the probability of yield disruption to the highest level since 2015–16. For BRSR Principle 6 climate-risk disclosure: the April 2026 numbers exceed the IPCC AR6 medium-emission scenario projections for this point in the cycle — meaning Indian companies running physical-risk scenario analysis on RCP 4.5 baselines are systematically underestimating exposure. For European insurers and reinsurers (Allianz, AXA, Munich Re, Swiss Re, Hannover Re): the second-consecutive year of record marine heatwave coverage hardens the actuarial case for sustained climate-risk reserve increases, particularly across coastal and agricultural lines. For COP31 (Antalya, November 2026): each monthly Copernicus bulletin closer to the conference adds urgency to the negotiating dynamics around production-side fossil-fuel commitments.
